Book Summary


The Graveyard Book

A fantasy novel written by Neil Gaiman. It tells the story of a young boy named Nobody Owens, also known as Bod, who is raised by ghosts in a graveyard after his family is murdered. The book follows Bod's adventures as he navigates the world of the living and the dead, encountering supernatural creatures and unraveling the mystery of his family's tragic fate. With the guidance of his ghostly guardians and the lessons he learns from the graveyard's inhabitants, Bod discovers his own identity and the power within him. It's a captivating tale of friendship, courage, and the search for belonging in a world filled with both darkness and light

A Wrinkle in Time

A science fantasy novel written by Madeleine L'Engle. The story revolves around a young girl named Meg Murry, her genius younger brother Charles Wallace, and their friend Calvin O'Keefe. They embark on a cosmic journey across time and space to rescue Meg's missing father, a scientist who disappeared while researching interdimensional travel. Assisted by three celestial beings known as Mrs. Whatsit, Mrs. Who, and Mrs. Which, the children travel through a tesseract—a wrinkle in time—to reach the planet Camazotz, where they confront the darkness known as IT. Meg must embrace her inner strength and love to save her father and ultimately triumph over evil. "A Wrinkle in Time" explores themes of courage, individuality, and the power of love in the face of darkness and conformity.



Quotations


The Graveyard Book

  • “I think ... I said things to Silas. He’ll be angry. ” “If he didn’t care about you, you couldn’t upset him, ” was all she said. (p. 198)

    • Voice One: The relationship between Bob and Silas is a testament to the depth and complexity of familial love. Despite the love and care that Silas provides, Bob worries about upsetting Silas and causing him to be angry. Silas's love for Bob is so strong that he would not become angry unless he truly cared about him.

    Voice Two: I used to have arguments with my parents over minor things, and whenever I heard hurtful words, it would bring me to tears. This situation highlights the special bond I share with my family. Despite the conflicts, there is a deep love and connection between us that sometimes even results in playing favorites with each other.

  • “Because there are mysteries. Because there are things that people are forbidden to speak about. Because there are things they do not remember.” ( p.164)

    • Voice One: Bod was upset that everyone refused to talk about the Macabray, a word used to describe something unpleasant. Silas taught Bod that people often avoid speaking about certain things as a way to get away with it. The author turned the word "Macabre" into "Macabray" to suggest that the celebration was actually the horrible things happened in reality

    Voice Two: My family don't talk about the deaths of family members, which has been confusing to me. When my grandpa died, I was unable to ask my parents about how he died, fearing he had been suffering for a long time. Despite this, I still don't know what happened and don't want to talk about it.

  • “We want you to surprise us and disappoint us and impress us and amaze us. Come home, Bod.” (p.198)

    • Voice One: We strive to meet others' expectations, but if someone tells us "I want you to disappoint us" we feel touched and encouraged to try.

A Wrinkle in Time

  • “Well, sure,” Calvin said. “Here. But it’s pretty complicated.” Meg smoothed out the paper and studied it. “Do they care how you do it?” She asked. “I mean, can you work it out your own way?” (p. 49)

    • Voice One: Teachers should encourage exploration and alternative solutions to problems, while acknowledging and rewarding unconventional thinking and problem-solving abilities, to promote a learning environment that nurtures growth and empowers students to become confident problem solvers.

    Voice Two: My math teacher had a harsh punishment policy for students who performed poorly or scored low grades, which led to me giving up on math. I wish I had encountered a teacher who appreciated different approaches to learning.

  • “We don’t know what things look like, as you say,” the beast said. “We know what things are like. It must be a very limiting thing, this seeing.” (p. 199)

    • Voice One: Vision is important for learning new things, but it can be shallow and lead to false categorization. We should be grateful for it, but it can also blind us to the true color of things and lead to false categorization.

    Voice Two: In kindergarten, there was a beautiful classmate who excelled in ballet, captivating everyone's admiration except for mine. Everyone were blinded by her beauty and failed to recognize her evil.

  • “Nobody suffers here,” Charles intoned. “Nobody is every unhappy.” “But nobody’s every happy, either,” Meg said earnestly. “Maybe if you aren’t unhappy sometimes you don’t know how to be happy. Calvin, I want to go home.” (p.157)

    • Voice One: Experiencing unhappiness is essential to truly understand and appreciate happiness, as a life devoid of suffering and challenges would eventually lead to a lack of genuine happiness.
